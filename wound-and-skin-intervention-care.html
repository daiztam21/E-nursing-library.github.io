Wound and Skin Intervention Care

Wound and skin integrity care is a critical component of nursing practice aimed at promoting tissue healing, preventing infections, and minimizing complications associated with skin injuries. The skin serves as the body's first line of defense, and any disruption can lead to severe health risks if not properly managed. Nurses play a vital role in assessing, treating, and monitoring wounds to ensure optimal recovery for patients.

This section provides comprehensive guidance on wound care, including wound classification, dressing techniques, advanced therapies, and prevention strategies. By understanding the principles of wound healing and skin integrity maintenance, nurses can implement evidence-based interventions to support patient recovery.

Types of Wounds and Stages of Healing

Purpose:

To classify wounds based on their characteristics and stages of healing, enabling appropriate treatment and management.

Procedure:

  1. Identify the wound type: Wounds can be classified as acute (surgical, traumatic) or chronic (pressure ulcers, diabetic ulcers).
  2. Assess the depth and severity: Superficial, partial-thickness, or full-thickness wounds.
  3. Determine the healing stage:
    • Inflammatory Phase (0-3 days): Redness, swelling, and pain as the body fights infection.
    • Proliferative Phase (4-21 days): Granulation tissue forms, and new blood vessels develop.
    • Maturation Phase (21 days to months): Collagen remodeling and scar formation occur.
  4. Implement appropriate wound care based on the healing stage.

Wound Dressing Techniques

Purpose:

To protect wounds, promote healing, and prevent infection by selecting and applying suitable dressings.

Procedure:

  1. Assess the wound: Determine moisture levels, exudate, and risk of infection.
  2. Select the appropriate dressing:
    • Dry wounds: Use hydrogels or occlusive dressings to maintain moisture.
    • Exudative wounds: Foam or alginate dressings absorb excess fluid.
    • Infected wounds: Antimicrobial dressings (silver or iodine-based) are recommended.
  3. Cleanse the wound with normal saline or an antiseptic solution.
  4. Apply the dressing using an aseptic technique.
  5. Secure the dressing and document wound progress.

Suture & Staple Removal

Purpose:

To safely remove sutures or staples once the wound has healed sufficiently, preventing infection and promoting proper closure.

Procedure:

  1. Confirm physician’s order for removal.
  2. Assess the wound for adequate healing (minimal redness, no drainage).
  3. Prepare sterile equipment (suture scissors, staple remover, antiseptic swabs).
  4. Remove sutures/staples carefully, one at a time, using aseptic technique.
  5. Apply steri-strips or adhesive bandages if necessary.
  6. Educate the patient on wound care and signs of complications.

Pressure Ulcer Prevention & Management

Purpose:

To prevent pressure injuries in immobile patients and manage existing ulcers to facilitate healing.

Procedure:

  1. Assess pressure points (sacrum, heels, elbows) for early signs of breakdown.
  2. Implement repositioning schedules (every 2 hours in bed, every 1 hour in a chair).
  3. Use pressure-relieving devices (foam mattresses, cushions).
  4. Maintain skin moisture balance—avoid excessive dryness or wetness.
  5. Apply appropriate dressings for existing ulcers (hydrocolloids, foam, or alginate).
  6. Monitor and document wound progress, adjusting interventions as needed.

Burn Wound Care

Purpose:

To manage burn injuries effectively, minimize complications, and promote wound healing.

Procedure:

  1. Assess burn severity (first-degree, second-degree, third-degree).
  2. Cleanse the wound with mild soap and water or prescribed solutions.
  3. Apply topical antimicrobial agents (silver sulfadiazine).
  4. Cover with appropriate dressings (non-adherent gauze, hydrogel).
  5. Monitor for signs of infection (redness, pus, fever).
  6. Provide pain management and fluid replacement as needed.

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT)

Purpose:

To enhance wound healing by applying controlled suction to remove exudate, reduce edema, and stimulate tissue growth.

Procedure:

  1. Assess wound suitability for NPWT (large, deep, or chronic wounds).
  2. Prepare the wound by cleaning and removing necrotic tissue if necessary.
  3. Apply a foam or gauze dressing over the wound.
  4. Attach the NPWT device and ensure an airtight seal.
  5. Set appropriate negative pressure settings as prescribed.
  6. Monitor the wound for progress and adjust therapy as needed.

Debridement Techniques

Purpose:

To remove necrotic, infected, or non-viable tissue to promote wound healing.

Procedure:

  1. Assess the wound and determine the most suitable debridement method:
    • Mechanical: Wet-to-dry dressings, irrigation.
    • Autolytic: Hydrogel or hydrocolloid dressings to allow the body to break down tissue.
