Medication Administration This section provides detailed medication safety and administration guidelines.

Oral Medication Administration

A. Administration of Tablets

  1. Verify the prescription and the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene before handling medications.
  3. Check the medication label three times:
    • Before taking the medication from storage.
    • While preparing the dose.
    • Before administering it to the patient.
  4. Prepare the medication:
    • Place the correct number of tablets in a medication cup.
    • o Do not crush enteric-coated or extended-release tablets unless specifically ordered.
  5. Identify the patient using two identifiers (e.g., name and date of birth).
  6. Explain the medication to the patient (name, purpose, and possible side effects).
  7. Assist the patient in taking the tablet:
    • Offer a glass of water unless contraindicated.
    • Ensure they swallow it completely.
  8. Observe for any adverse reactions after administration.
  9. Document the medication administration, including time, dose, and patient response.

B. Administration of Capsules

  1. Verify the prescription and follow the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene before handling the medication.
  3. Check the medication label three times as done for tablets.
  4. Prepare the medication:
    • Place the prescribed number of capsules into a medication cup.
    • Do not open, break, or crush capsules unless ordered by the physician.
  5. Identify the patient and confirm any allergies.
  6. Explain the medication to the patient.
  7. Assist the patient in taking the capsule:
    • Provide water to help swallow the capsule unless contraindicated.
    • Ensure the patient does not chew or open the capsule unless instructed.
  8. Observe for any adverse reactions.
  9. Document the medication administration properly.

C. Administration of Liquid Medications

  1. Verify the prescription and follow the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene before handling the medication.
  3. Check the medication label three times as with tablets and capsules.
  4. Prepare the liquid medication:
    • Shake the bottle well if required (e.g., suspensions).
    • Use a calibrated measuring cup or oral syringe for accuracy.
    • Pour the liquid at eye level to ensure the correct dose.
    • Wipe the bottle lip before closing to prevent contamination.
  5. Identify the patient and confirm any allergies.
  6. Explain the medication to the patient.
  7. Assist the patient in taking the liquid:
    • Have the patient drink it directly from the measuring cup or oral syringe.
    • Offer water afterward if allowed, to wash away any residual taste. .
  8. Observe for any adverse reactions.
  9. Document the medication administration properly.

Setting Up IV Lines and Administering IV Medications

Purpose:

  • To ensure safe and effective administration of IV fluids and medications.
  • To maintain IV site integrity and prevent complications.
  • To provide proper care and monitoring of IV lines.

A. Procedure for Setting Up IV Lines

  1. Verify the physician’s order for IV therapy (fluid type, rate, and any medications to be infused).
  2. Perform hand hygiene and prepare all necessary equipment.
  3. Identify the patient using two identifiers.
  4. Explain the procedure to the patient to gain cooperation.
  5. Prepare the IV solution and tubing:
    • Check the expiration date and integrity of the IV fluid bag.
    • Prime the IV tubing by allowing fluid to flow through to remove air bubbles.
  6. Select an appropriate IV insertion site (e.g., forearm, dorsum of hand).
  7. Apply a tourniquet above the selected site to make veins more visible.
  8. Clean the site using an antiseptic solution and allow it to dry.
  9. Insert the IV catheter at the proper angle (10-30° depending on vein depth).
  10. Confirm blood return to ensure the catheter is in the vein, then advance fully.
  11. Remove the tourniquet and secure the catheter with tape and a transparent dressing.
  12. Connect the IV tubing and set the infusion rate as prescribed.
  13. Monitor the patient for any signs of discomfort, infiltration, or allergic reaction.
  14. Document the procedure, including site, gauge, and patient response.

Procedure for Administering IV Medications

  1. Verify the prescription and the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Check for IV patency by flushing with normal saline.
  3. Prepare the medication following aseptic technique.
  4. Administer the medication via the correct method:
    • IV push (bolus): Inject the medication slowly at the recommended rate.
    • IV piggyback (secondary infusion): Attach the medication bag to the primary IV line and regulate flow.
  5. Monitor the patient for any adverse reactions.
  6. Flush the IV line if required after medication administration.
  7. Document the medication administration and patient response.

B. Flushing and Closing IV Lines

Flushing an IV Line

  1. Perform hand hygiene and wear gloves.
  2. Verify the IV patency before flushing.
  3. Prepare a syringe with normal saline (or heparin if required).
  4. Clean the IV port with an antiseptic wipe.
  5. Attach the syringe and inject the saline using a slow, pulsatile push technique.
  6. Observe for resistance or leakage. If resistance is noted, do not force the flush.
  7. Disconnect the syringe and ensure the line remains clear.

Closing an IV Line

  1. Stop the IV infusion if discontinuing therapy.
  2. Flush the line with normal saline (or heparin if required).
  3. Clamp the IV tubing to prevent air from entering.
  4. Remove the IV catheter if discontinuing IV therapy.
  5. Apply pressure to the site with sterile gauze for 1-2 minutes.
  6. Cover the site with a sterile dressing and check for bleeding.
  7. Dispose of the catheter properly and remove gloves.
  8. Document the procedure, including the time of IV discontinuation and the condition of the site.

C. IV Site Care and Troubleshooting Complications

Routine IV Site Care

  1. Inspect the IV site regularly for redness, swelling, or leakage.
  2. Check for proper IV flow and adjust if needed.
  3. Change IV dressings and tubing per hospital protocol.
  4. Keep the IV site clean and dry to prevent infection.

Troubleshooting Common IV Complications

  • Infiltration occurs when IV fluid leaks into the surrounding tissue, causing swelling, cool skin, pallor, and pain. If this happens, stop the infusion immediately, elevate the limb, apply a warm compress, and restart the IV in a different site.
  • Phlebitis is inflammation of the vein, usually indicated by redness, warmth, pain, and swelling along the vein. Treatment involves stopping the IV, applying a warm compress, and restarting the IV in a new site.
  • Extravasation happens when a vesicant drug leaks into the tissue, leading to severe pain, redness, and possible blistering. The IV infusion should be stopped immediately, the drug should be aspirated if possible, and an antidote should be administered if available. The site should be monitored for tissue damage.
  • Infection at the IV site presents as redness, warmth, pus formation, and fever. If infection is suspected, the IV should be removed, a culture of the site should be obtained, and antibiotics should be administered as necessary.
  • Air Embolism can occur if air enters the bloodstream, causing sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, and a drop in blood pressure. If suspected, the IV should be clamped, the patient should be placed in the Trendelenburg position, and oxygen should be administered while notifying the physician.
  • Occlusion is when the IV line becomes blocked, leading to slow or stopped IV flow and resistance when flushing. To address this, the tubing should be checked for kinks, and a gentle flush should be attempted. If resistance persists, the IV line should not be forced, and an alternative site should be considered.

Injection Techniques

Purpose:

  • To administer medications safely and effectively via different injection routes.
  • To ensure proper absorption of the medication.
  • To minimize discomfort and prevent complications.

A. Subcutaneous Injection

Definition: A subcutaneous (SC) injection delivers medication into the fatty tissue beneath the skin, allowing for slow absorption.

Common Sites:

  • Outer aspect of the upper arm
  • Abdomen (at least 2 inches away from the navel)
  • Anterior thigh
  • Upper buttocks

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and prepare the medication.
  3. Select an appropriate injection site and assess for any bruising, infection, or lipodystrophy.
  4. Clean the injection site with an antiseptic wipe and allow it to dry.
  5. Pinch the skin to lift the fatty tissue.
  6. Insert the needle at a 45° to 90° angle, depending on the patient's body fat.
  7. Inject the medication slowly and steadily.
  8. Withdraw the needle quickly and apply gentle pressure with gauze (do not massage).
  9. Dispose of the needle in a sharps container.
  10. Document the administration, including the site and patient’s response.

B. Intramuscular Injection

Definition: An intramuscular (IM) injection delivers medication directly into the muscle for faster absorption.

Common Sites:

  • Deltoid muscle (upper arm) – for small volumes (≤1 mL)
  • Vastus lateralis muscle (thigh) – preferred site for infants
  • Ventrogluteal muscle (hip) – preferred site for adults
  • Dorsogluteal muscle (buttocks) – less commonly used due to risk of sciatic nerve injury

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and prepare the medication.
  3. Select the injection site, ensuring it is free from redness, swelling, or scars.
  4. Clean the area with an antiseptic wipe and allow it to dry.
  5. Stretch the skin taut to ensure proper muscle penetration.
  6. Insert the needle at a 90° angle using a swift, dart-like motion.
  7. Aspirate (if required) to check for blood return (see section E for guidance).
  8. Inject the medication slowly and steadily.
  9. Withdraw the needle quickly and apply pressure with gauze.
  10. Dispose of the needle in a sharps container.
  11. Document the administration, including the site and patient’s response.

C. Intradermal Injection

Definition: An intradermal (ID) injection is administered into the dermis, just below the epidermis, for diagnostic testing or local anesthesia.

Common Sites:

  • Inner forearm
  • Upper back
  • Upper chest

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and prepare the medication.
  3. Select an appropriate site and ensure it is free from irritation or scarring.
  4. Clean the skin with an antiseptic wipe and allow it to dry.
  5. Hold the skin taut with your non-dominant hand.
  6. Insert the needle at a 5° to 15° angle, bevel up, just under the skin.
  7. Inject the medication slowly until a small wheal (raised bump) forms.
  8. Withdraw the needle carefully and do not massage the area.
  9. Dispose of the needle in a sharps container.
  10. Document the administration, including the site and patient’s response.

D. Z-Track Method

Definition:

The Z-track technique is a variation of the intramuscular injection method used to prevent medication from leaking into the subcutaneous tissue, commonly used for irritating or staining medications (e.g., iron dextran).

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and prepare the medication.
  3. Select the ventrogluteal or vastus lateralis site, avoiding the deltoid.
  4. Clean the injection site with an antiseptic wipe and allow it to dry.
  5. Pull the skin laterally (about 1-1.
    6. 5 inches) to displace the tissue.
  6. Insert the needle at a 90° angle into the muscle.
  7. Aspirate if required, then inject the medication slowly.
  8. Wait 10 seconds before withdrawing the needle to allow the medication to settle.
  9. Withdraw the needle quickly and release the displaced skin immediately to seal the track.
  10. Do not massage the area to prevent medication from leaking into the subcutaneous tissue.
  11. Dispose of the needle in a sharps container.
  12. Document the administration, including the site and patient’s response.

E. Aspiration and Non-Aspiration Techniques

Aspiration Technique:

  • Aspiration refers to pulling back the plunger before injecting medication to check for blood return, which indicates if the needle is in a blood vessel.
  • It is traditionally performed for intramuscular injections in larger muscles (gluteal sites).
  • Current practice varies, and aspiration is often not required for deltoid and vastus lateralis injections.

Non-Aspiration Technique:

  • Many guidelines suggest not aspirating when administering vaccines or medications in non-high-risk areas like the deltoid.
  • For subcutaneous and intradermal injections, aspiration is not needed.
  • The Z-track method does not require aspiration in most cases.

When to Aspirate and When Not To:

Aspirate for:

  • Intramuscular injections in the dorsogluteal and ventrogluteal sites (some protocols still recommend it).
  • Injectable medications that require confirmation of muscle placement (e.g., certain antibiotics or oil-based
    • medications).

Do NOT aspirate for:

  • Subcutaneous and intradermal injections.
  • Intramuscular injections in the deltoid and vastus lateralis.
  • Vaccines, insulin, heparin, and anticoagulants.

Nebulization & Respiratory Treatments

Purpose:

  • To ensure proper delivery of medication to the respiratory system.
  • To improve breathing in patients with respiratory conditions (e.g., asthma, COPD).
  • To educate patients on the correct use of respiratory devices.

A. Proper Use of Nebulizers

Definition:

A nebulizer is a device that converts liquid medication into a fine mist for inhalation, ensuring direct delivery to the lungs.

Indications:

  • Asthma
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
  • Bronchitis
  • Respiratory infections requiring bronchodilators or corticosteroids

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the prescribed medication.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and gather the necessary equipment:
  3. Nebulizer machine
  4. Medication (pre-filled vial or dose to be mixed with saline)
  5. Mouthpiece or face mask
  6. Tubing and nebulizer cup
  7. Prepare the medication:
    • If using a pre-filled vial, twist open and pour into the nebulizer cup.
    • If mixing medication, use the prescribed amount of saline.
  8. Assemble the nebulizer by connecting the tubing, mouthpiece/mask, and nebulizer cup.
  9. Instruct the patient to sit upright in a comfortable position.
  10. Turn on the nebulizer machine and check for a steady mist.
  11. Instruct the patient to inhale deeply and slowly through the mouthpiece or mask, holding each breath for a few seconds before exhaling.
  12. Continue treatment until all medication is used (usually 5-10 minutes).
  13. Turn off the machine and disconnect the nebulizer set.
  14. Clean the nebulizer components according to manufacturer guidelines.
  15. Monitor the patient’s response and document the procedure, including any adverse reactions.

B. Proper Use of Metered-Dose Inhalers (MDIs)

Definition:

A metered-dose inhaler (MDI) is a handheld device that delivers a precise dose of aerosolized medication into the lungs.

Indications:

  • Asthma
  • COPD
  • Bronchospasm management

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and ensure the correct medication.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and prepare the inhaler.
  3. Shake the inhaler well (5-10 seconds) to mix the medication.
  4. Remove the cap and attach a spacer if prescribed (spacers improve medication delivery).
  5. Instruct the patient to exhale fully, removing as much air as possible from the lungs.
  6. Place the inhaler mouthpiece between the lips, forming a tight seal.
  7. Press down on the inhaler to release one dose while the patient inhales deeply and slowly.
  8. Instruct the patient to hold their breath for 10 seconds before exhaling slowly.
  9. Wait at least 30 seconds to 1 minute before taking a second dose, if prescribed
  10. If using a corticosteroid inhaler, instruct the patient to rinse their mouth after use to prevent oral thrush.
  11. Clean the inhaler mouthpiece regularly and store it properly.
  12. Monitor the patient’s technique and response and document the administration.

Transdermal & Topical Medications

Purpose:

  • To ensure proper absorption of medications applied to the skin.
  • To provide localized or systemic therapeutic effects.
  • To minimize side effects associated with oral or injectable medications.

A. Application of Medicated Patches

Definition:

A transdermal patch is a medicated adhesive applied to the skin to deliver a controlled dose of medication over time.

Common Uses:

  • Pain relief (e.g., fentanyl, lidocaine)
  • Hormone therapy (e.g., estrogen, contraceptive patches)
  • Cardiovascular conditions (e.g., nitroglycerin)
  • Smoking cessation (e.g., nicotine patches)

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the prescribed medication.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and gather the necessary materials.
  3. Assess the patient’s skin for irritation, rashes, or previous patch application sites.
  4. Select a clean, dry, hairless area (avoid broken or irritated skin).
  5. Remove the old patch (if applicable), fold it in half, and dispose of it properly.
  6. Clean the skin with mild soap and water (avoid alcohol-based cleansers that may cause irritation).
  7. Open the new patch packaging and remove the protective liner without touching the adhesive side.
  8. Apply the patch firmly to the selected site, pressing for about 10 seconds.
  9. Ensure the edges are well-adhered and instruct the patient to avoid excessive sweating or water exposure.
  10. Label the patch with the date, time, and initials if required.
  11. Document the administration, site of application, and patient’s response.

B. Applying Creams, Ointments, and Lotions

Definition:

Topical medications include creams, ointments, and lotions applied directly to the skin for localized treatment.

Common Uses:

  • Skin conditions (e.g., eczema, psoriasis, fungal infections)
  • Pain relief (e.g., capsaicin, diclofenac)
  • Antibiotic treatments (e.g., mupirocin for wounds)

Procedure:

  1. Verify the physician’s order and check the prescribed medication.
  2. Perform hand hygiene and wear gloves to prevent contamination.
  3. Assess the patient’s skin for irritation, infection, or open wounds.
  4. Clean the affected area with mild soap and water, then pat dry.
  5. Apply a thin layer of medication using a gloved hand, sterile applicator, or cotton swab (avoid using bare hands).
  6. Rub in the medication gently, unless otherwise directed.
  7. For occlusive therapy, cover the area with a sterile dressing if prescribed.
  8. Remove gloves properly and perform hand hygiene.
  9. Monitor for allergic reactions or side effects such as redness, itching, or burning.
  10. Document the application, site, and patient’s response.

Medication Error Prevention & Safety

Purpose:

  • To ensure safe and accurate medication administration.
  • To prevent medication errors that may cause harm to the patient.
  • To promote patient safety and adherence to proper protocols.

A. The 10 Rights of Medication Administration

The 10 Rights of Medication Administration serve as a fundamental guideline for nurses and healthcare providers to prevent errors and ensure patient safety.

  1. Right Patient
    • Verify the patient’s identity using two identifiers (e.g., full name, birthdate, hospital ID number).
    • Ask the patient to state their name whenever possible.
    • Check the patient’s identification band before administering medication.
  2. Right Drug
    • Confirm the correct medication name (generic and brand, if applicable).
    • Check the expiration date and appearance of the medication.
    • Ensure the prescribed drug is appropriate for the patient’s condition.
  3. Right Dose
    • Verify the prescribed dosage strength and calculations.
    • Use appropriate measuring tools (syringes, droppers, or calibrated cups).
    • Be cautious with high-alert medications (e.g., insulin, anticoagulants).
  4. Right Route
    • Confirm the prescribed route (oral, IV, IM, SC, topical, inhalation, etc.).
    • Ensure the patient can tolerate the route (e.g., checking for swallowing ability in oral medications).
  5. Right Time
    • Administer the medication at the prescribed time and frequency.
    • Be mindful of medications requiring specific timing (e.g., before or after meals, insulin before food intake).
    • Maintain a consistent schedule to ensure therapeutic effectiveness.
  6. Right Documentation
    • Record the medication name, dose, route, time, and patient response immediately after administration.
    • Note any refusals, missed doses, or adverse reactions.
    • Use standardized documentation formats (e.g., FDAR, SOAP).
  7. Right Education
    • Inform the patient about the purpose, dose, route, and side effects of the medication.
    • Teach the patient proper self-administration techniques for home medications.
    • Address any concerns or questions the patient may have.
  8. Right Evaluation
    • Assess the patient’s response to the medication (improvement or adverse effects).
    • Monitor vital signs, pain levels, or lab results as necessary.
    • Report and document any side effects or unexpected reactions.
  9. Right to Refuse
    • Recognize the patient’s right to refuse medication.
    • Explain the importance and consequences of the medication.
    • Document the refusal and any related patient concerns.
    • Inform the physician if refusal may impact the patient’s health.
  10. Right Assessment
    • Perform pre-administration assessments (e.g., checking BP before antihypertensives, blood glucose before insulin).
    • Consider patient allergies, contraindications, and medical history.
    • Verify laboratory results that may affect medication safety.

Patient Education on Medications

Purpose:

  • To ensure patients understand how to take their medications correctly.
  • To prevent medication errors, adverse reactions, and drug interactions.
  • To improve medication adherence and patient outcomes.

A. Teaching Patients About Medication Adherence

Definition:

Medication adherence refers to taking medications as prescribed—correct dose, time, frequency, and duration—to achieve the intended therapeutic effect.

Teaching Points:

  • Explain the importance of adherence – Emphasize how taking medication correctly improves health and prevents complications.
  • Provide clear instructions – Teach patients when, how, and for how long to take their medications (e.g., with or without food, morning vs. night).
  • Encourage reminders – Suggest pill organizers, alarms, or mobile apps to help track medication schedules.
  • Address common barriers – Discuss cost, forgetfulness, difficulty swallowing, or side effects that may lead to non-adherence.
  • Explain what to do if a dose is missed – Advise patients on whether to take a missed dose immediately or wait for the next scheduled dose.
  • Encourage open communication – Urge patients to inform their healthcare provider if they are experiencing issues with their medication regimen.

B. Teaching Patients About Side Effects and Interactions

Medication Side Effects

Definition:

Side effects are unintended reactions to a medication, which can range from mild to severe.


Teaching Points:

  • Identify common side effects (e.g., nausea, dizziness, fatigue) and ways to manage them.
  • Explain severe side effects (e.g., difficulty breathing, swelling, rash) and when to seek immediate medical attention.
  • Encourage reporting of unusual symptoms to a healthcare provider rather than stopping medication abruptly.
  • Suggest lifestyle modifications to minimize discomfort (e.g., taking food with medications that cause nausea).

Medication Interactions

Definition:

Medication interactions occur when a drug affects the way another drug, food, or supplement works, potentially causing harmful effects.


Teaching Points:

  • Discuss common drug-drug interactions, such as antibiotics reducing birth control effectiveness.
  • Educate about food interactions, such as avoiding grapefruit with certain cholesterol medications.
  • Advise caution with herbal supplements – Some herbs can interfere with prescription drugs.
  • Encourage full medication disclosure – Patients should inform their doctor about all medications, including over- the-counter and herbal products.

Medication Documentation & Charting

Purpose:

  • To ensure accurate and complete records of medication administration.
  • To promote continuity of care and patient safety.
  • To provide legal documentation of nursing interventions.

A. FDAR Charting for Medication Administration

Definition:


FDAR is a problem-oriented documentation format used to record patient care in a structured manner. It ensures that all relevant details about the patient’s condition, interventions, and responses are clearly documented.

Components of FDAR:

F – Focus

  • The main issue or observation that requires attention.
  • It can be a sign or symptom, medical condition, nursing diagnosis, intervention, or patient concern.
  • Example: "Pain management," "Medication refusal," "Hypertension," or "Adverse drug reaction."

D – Data

  • Includes both subjective (patient-reported) and objective (clinically observed) findings.
  • Examples:
    • "Patient reports pain at 7/10 in the lower back."
    • "Vital signs: BP 140/90 mmHg, HR 95 bpm."
    • "Patient refused to take medication, stating it made them feel nauseous."

A – Action

  • Describes the nursing intervention or action taken to address the issue.
  • Examples:
    • "Administered Paracetamol 500mg per os as prescribed."
    • "Educated the patient about the importance of taking antihypertensive medication."
    • "Encouraged deep breathing exercises to help with dizziness."

R – Response

  • The patient’s reaction or outcome after the intervention.
  • Examples:
    • "Patient verbalized relief; pain level decreased to 3/10 after 30 minutes."
    • "Patient still hesitant to take medication but agreed after further explanation."
    • "BP lowered to 130/85 mmHg after one hour; patient reports feeling better."

Example FDAR Entry for Medication Administration:

F: Pain management
D: Patient reports pain at 7/10 in the lower back. Facial grimacing observed.
A: Administered Paracetamol 500mg per os as prescribed. Provided comfort measures.
R: Patient verbalized relief; pain level decreased to 3/10 after 30 minutes.

B. SOAP Charting for Medication Administration

Definition:

SOAP is a structured documentation format used for systematic problem-solving. It organizes information in a clear and logical sequence for effective communication among healthcare providers.

Components of SOAP:

S – Subjective

  • The patient’s verbal complaint or reported symptoms.
  • Includes the patient’s own words, pain level, discomfort, or concerns.
  • Example: "I have a headache and feel dizzy."

O – Objective

  • Measurable observations such as vital signs, lab results, physical exam findings, and nurse observations.
  • Examples:
    • "BP 130/85 mmHg, HR 78 bpm."
    • "Patient appears pale and diaphoretic."
    • "No signs of distress noted."

A – Assessment

  • The nurse’s interpretation of the patient’s condition based on the subjective and objective findings.
  • Example:
    • "Mild headache likely due to dehydration."
    • "Possible hypoglycemia due to skipped meal."

P – Plan

  • The nursing intervention or action to be taken for the patient’s condition.
  • Examples:
    • "Administered Ibuprofen 200mg per os."
    • "Encouraged oral fluid intake."
    • "Will reassess in 30 minutes."

Example SOAP Entry for Medication Administration:

S: "I have a headache and feel dizzy," patient states.
O: BP 130/85 mmHg, HR 78 bpm. No signs of distress.
A: Mild headache likely due to dehydration.
P: Administered Ibuprofen 200mg per os. Encouraged oral fluid intake. Will reassess in 30 minutes.

General Documentation Guidelines:

  • Use clear, concise, and factual language.
  • Record immediately after medication administration.
  • Include drug name, dosage, route, time given, and patient’s response.
  • Document any adverse reactions, refusals, or missed doses.
  • Use approved abbreviations and terminology for clarity and accuracy.
