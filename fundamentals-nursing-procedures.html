Fundamental Nursing Procedures

Handwashing for surgical procedures

The purpose of surgical hand scrub is to:

  1. Remove debris and transient microorganisms from the nails, hands, and forearms,
  2. Reduce the resident microbial count to a minimum, and
  3. Inhibit rapid rebound growth of microorganism

The procedure for the timed scrub consists of:

  1. Remove all jewelry (rings, watches, bracelets).
  2. Wash hands and arms with anitmicrobial soap. Excessively hot water is harder on the skin, dries the skin, and is too uncomfortable to wash with for the recommended amount of time. However, because cold water prevents soap from lathering properly, soil and germs may not be washed away.
  3. Clean subungual areas with a nail file.
  4. Start timing. Scrub each side of each finger, between the fingers, and the back and front of the hand for two minutes.
  5. Proceed to scrub the arms, keeping the hand higher than the arm at all times. This prevents bacteria-laden soap and water from contaminating the hand.
  6. Wash each side of the arm to three inches above the elbow for one minute.
  7. Repeat the process on the other hand and arm, keeping hands above elbows at all times. If the hand touches anything except the brush at any time, the scrub must be lengthened by one minute for the area that has been contaminated.
  8. Rinse hands and arms by passing them through the water in one direction only, from fingertips to elbow. Do not move the arm back and forth through the water.
  9. Proceed to the operating room suite holding hands above elbows.
  10. If the hands and arms are grossly soiled, the scrub time should be lengthened. However, vigorous scrubbing that causes the skin to become abraded should be avoided.
  11. At all times during the scrub procedure care should be taken not to splash water onto surgical attire.
  12. Once in the operating room suite, hands and arms should be dried using a sterile towel and aseptic technique. You are now ready to don your gown and sterile gloves.

Handwashing for routine patient care (Medical Handwashing)

  1. Remove jewelry
    - Take off rings and watches before washing hands.
  2. Turn on clean running water
    - Use warm or cool water. Avoid touching the faucet with clean hands if possible (use elbow or paper towel if needed).
  3. Wet your hands thoroughly
    - Wet all surfaces of your hands and wrists.
  4. Apply soap
    - Use enough soap to cover all hand surfaces.
  5. Rub hands palm to palm
    - Create a good lather.
  6. Rub the back of each hand with the palm of the other hand
    - Interlace fingers.
  7. Rub palm to palm with fingers interlaced
  8. Rub the backs of fingers to opposing palms with fingers interlocked
  9. Clean each thumb using rotational rubbing
  10. Rub fingertips in circular motion on palm
    - Clean under the nails.
  11. Rub each wrist with the opposite hand
  12. Rinse hands thoroughly
    - Rinse from wrists down to fingertips.
  13. Dry hands with a clean paper towel or air dryer
    - Do not reuse towels.
  14. Turn off faucet using paper towel (if applicable)
    - Avoid recontaminating clean hands.

Donning and doffing clean gloves

Donning (Putting On) Clean Gloves:

  1. Perform hand hygiene
    - Wash hands with soap and water or use alcohol-based hand sanitizer.
  2. Choose the correct glove size
    - Gloves should fit snugly but comfortably.
  3. Inspect gloves for damage
    - Check for tears or holes before putting them on.
  4. Put on the first glove
    - Hold the glove at the cuff and insert your hand carefully.
  5. Put on the second glove
    - Slide the second hand into the glove without touching the outside of the first glove.
  6. Adjust gloves if needed
    - Ensure full coverage of the hands and wrists without overstretching the gloves.

Doffing (Removing) Clean Gloves:

  1. Grasp the outside of one glove at the wrist (without touching your skin)
    - Peel it away from your hand, turning it inside out.
  2. Hold the removed glove in the gloved hand
  3. Slide fingers of the ungloved hand under the wrist of the remaining glove
    - Avoid touching the outside surface.
  4. Peel the second glove off over the first glove
    - This will trap the first glove inside the second one.
  5. Dispose of gloves properly
    - Discard in a proper waste bin.
  6. Perform hand hygiene again
    - Wash hands or use hand sanitizer immediately after glove removal.

Donning and doffing sterile gloves

Donning Sterile Gloves (Open Gloving Technique):

  1. Perform hand hygiene
    - Wash hands thoroughly and dry with a clean towel or air dry.
  2. Open the sterile glove package
    - Place the inner wrapper on a clean, dry surface.
    - Open it carefully to avoid contaminating the gloves.
  3. Pick up the first glove by the inside cuff
    - Use your non-dominant hand to pick up the glove by the folded cuff (inner side).
    - Touch only the inside of the glove.
  4. Slide your dominant hand into the glove
    - Keep your fingers pointed upward and avoid touching any outer surface.
  5. Put on the second glove
    - Slide your gloved fingers under the cuff of the second glove.
    - Lift the glove and slide your non-dominant hand in, being careful not to touch the outer surface of either glove.
  6. Adjust as needed
    - Once both gloves are on, you may adjust fingers and cuffs by touching only sterile-to-sterile surfaces.

Doffing Sterile Gloves:

  1. Grasp the outside of one glove at the wrist
    - Do not touch your skin. Peel the glove away, turning it inside out.
  2. Hold the removed glove in your gloved hand
  3. Slide fingers under the wrist of the remaining glove
    - With your bare hand, slip fingers inside the cuff of the remaining glove (touching only the inside).
  4. Peel off the second glove over the first one
    - This traps the first glove inside. Dispose of gloves in a proper biohazard bin.
  5. Perform hand hygiene again
    - Wash hands thoroughly or use alcohol-based hand rub.

Gowning and gloving for sterile procedures

Gowning (Putting on a Sterile Gown):

  1. Open the sterile gown pack
    - Carefully unfold it on a sterile field without touching the outer surface of the gown.
  2. Pick up the gown by the inside neck area
    - Touch only the inner surface; let it unfold by itself.
  3. Let the gown unfold fully
    - Hold it away from your body and allow it to drop without touching any non-sterile surface.
  4. Insert arms into sleeves
    - Do not push hands out through the cuffs yet—just insert arms up to the cuff edge.
  5. Wait for the circulating nurse to assist with gowning
    - The nurse will pull the gown over your shoulders and tie or secure the neck and waist at the back.

Closed Gloving (Sterile Gloves Over Gown):

  1. Pick up the first glove
    - With your hand inside the gown sleeve, grasp the folded cuff of the glove (palm down) with the gown-covered hand.
  2. Place glove on gown sleeve cuff
    - Align the glove’s cuff with the gown cuff, and carefully slide the glove over your hand and gown cuff while keeping your hand inside the gown.
  3. Pull glove on while pushing hand through cuff
    - As you pull the glove on, your hand should enter the glove and come out fully gloved.
  4. Repeat with the second glove
    - Use the now-gloved hand to pick up and apply the second glove in the same manner.
  5. Adjust gloves as needed
    - Touch only sterile surfaces (glove to glove).

After Gowning and Gloving:

- Keep hands above waist and in front of your body.
- Do not touch anything non-sterile.

Normal vital signs for adults, pediatric patients, and newborns

  1. Normal Vital Signs for Adults (18 years and older):
    • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C (97.7°F to 99.5°F)
    • Heart Rate (Pulse): 60 to 100 beats per minute
    • Respiratory Rate: 12 to 20 breaths per minute
    • Blood Pressure: 90/60 mmHg to 120/80 mmHg
    • Oxygen Saturation (SpO2): 95% or higher
  2. Normal Vital Signs for Pediatric Patients (Children):
      Infants (1 month to 12 months):
      • Heart Rate: 100 to 160 beats per minute
      • Respiratory Rate: 30 to 60 breaths per minute
      • Blood Pressure: 70/50 mmHg to 100/65 mmHg
      • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C
      • Oxygen Saturation: 95% or higher
      Toddlers (1 to 3 years):
      • Heart Rate: 90 to 150 beats per minute
      • Respiratory Rate: 24 to 40 breaths per minute
      • Blood Pressure: 80/55 mmHg to 110/70 mmHg
      • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C
      • Oxygen Saturation: 95% or higher
      Preschoolers (3 to 5 years):
      • Heart Rate: 80 to 140 beats per minute
      • Respiratory Rate: 22 to 34 breaths per minute
      • Blood Pressure: 85/55 mmHg to 115/70 mmHg
      • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C
      • Oxygen Saturation: 95% or higher
      School-age Children (6 to 12 years):
      • Heart Rate: 70 to 120 beats per minute
      • Respiratory Rate: 18 to 30 breaths per minute
      • Blood Pressure: 90/60 mmHg to 120/75 mmHg
      • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C
      • Oxygen Saturation: 95% or higher
      Adolescents (13 to 17 years):
      • Heart Rate: 60 to 100 beats per minute
      • Respiratory Rate: 12 to 20 breaths per minute
      • Blood Pressure: 100/70 mmHg to 120/80 mmHg
      • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C
      • Oxygen Saturation: 95% or higher
  3. Normal Vital Signs for Newborns (Birth to 1 Month):
    • Temperature: 36.5°C to 37.5°C (usually taken via axillary route)
    • Heart Rate: 120 to 160 beats per minute (may increase when crying)
    • Respiratory Rate: 30 to 60 breaths per minute
    • Blood Pressure: 60/30 mmHg to 90/60 mmHg
    • Oxygen Saturation (SpO2): 95% or higher (once stabilized after birth)

Techniques for measuring blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, and respiratory rate

  1. Blood Pressure (BP):

    Equipment: Sphygmomanometer (manual or digital), stethoscope (for manual), blood pressure cuff

    Technique (Manual):
    1. Ask the patient to sit or lie down and rest for 5 minutes.
    2. Ensure the arm is supported at heart level, palm facing up.
    3. Wrap the cuff snugly around the upper arm, 1 inch above the elbow crease.
    4. Place the stethoscope over the brachial artery (inside of the elbow).
    5. Inflate the cuff until about 20–30 mmHg above the expected systolic pressure.
    6. Slowly deflate the cuff (2–3 mmHg per second) while listening for:
      • First sound (systolic pressure): when you first hear the heartbeat
      • Last sound (diastolic pressure): when the sound disappears
    7. Record the reading as systolic over diastolic (e.g., 120/80 mmHg).
    8. If using a digital BP monitor, follow device instructions and ensure the cuff is correctly placed.
  2. Heart Rate (Pulse):

    Sites: Radial artery (wrist), carotid artery (neck), apical pulse (chest), or other accessible pulse points

    Technique:
    1. Use your index and middle fingers (not the thumb).
    2. Press lightly over the pulse point (most commonly radial or carotid).
    3. Count the beats for 30 seconds and multiply by 2 (if regular).
    4. Count for a full 60 seconds if the pulse is irregular.
    5. Note the rate (bpm), rhythm (regular or irregular), and strength (bounding, weak, etc.).
  3. Temperature:

      Sites: Oral, axillary (armpit), rectal, tympanic (ear), or temporal (forehead) Techniques by site:

    • Oral: Use a digital thermometer. Place under the tongue, close the mouth, wait until the beep.
      • Normal: 36.5°C – 37.5°C
    • Axillary: Place under the armpit with the arm held close to the body.
      • Usually 0.5°C lower than oral.
    • Rectal: Insert a lubricated thermometer gently into the rectum (~1 inch for adults).
      • Usually 0.5°C higher than oral.
    • Tympanic (Ear): Insert the thermometer gently into the ear canal and wait for the reading.
    • Temporal (Forehead): Swipe the thermometer across the forehead or press and hold at the temple. Always use the proper probe cover and clean the thermometer after each use.
  4. Respiratory Rate: Technique:
    1. Observe the patient without telling them you’re counting respirations (they may change their breathing if they know).
    2. Watch the rise and fall of the chest or place your hand on the chest or back.
    3. Count breaths for 30 seconds and multiply by 2 (if regular).
    4. Count for 60 seconds if breathing is irregular.
    5. Note the rate (breaths per minute), rhythm (regular/irregular), and effort (labored, shallow, deep, etc.).

Body mechanics and ergonomics for nurses

What is Body Mechanics?

- Body mechanics refers to the proper use of the body to facilitate movement and prevent injury, especially to the spine and muscles. It includes posture, alignment, balance, and coordinated movement.

What is Ergonomics?

- Ergonomics is the science of designing the job, equipment, and work environment to fit the worker—minimizing strain, fatigue, and risk of injury.

Principles of Proper Body Mechanics:

  1. Keep a wide base of support
    ● Stand with feet shoulder-width apart for balance.
  2. Use your legs, not your back
    ● Bend at your knees and hips when lifting—not your waist.
  3. Keep the load close to your body
    ● The closer the object is, the less strain on your muscles.
  4. Maintain a neutral spine
    ● Avoid twisting or bending sideways; keep your back straight.
  5. Push rather than pull
    ● Pushing requires less effort and reduces risk of injury.
  6. Avoid overreaching
    ● Move closer to the object or patient.
  7. Use smooth, coordinated movements
    ● Jerky movements can cause strain or injury.

Ergonomic Tips for Nurses:

  1. Adjust the height of the bed
    ● Raise the bed to waist level when performing procedures to avoid bending.
  2. Use assistive devices
    ● Transfer belts, slide boards, patient lifts, and walkers can help prevent strain.
  3. Teamwork when lifting heavy patients
    ● Always ask for assistance when needed.
  4. Alternate tasks and take micro-breaks
    ● Reduce repetitive strain and muscle fatigue by changing positions regularly.
  5. Use ergonomic chairs and equipment
    ● Ensure proper back support when sitting, especially during long charting sessions.

Wear supportive footwear

  • Non-slip, cushioned shoes reduce stress on joints and prevent falls.

Organize your workspace

  • Keep supplies within reach to avoid unnecessary bending or twisting.

    • Common High-Risk Tasks for Nurses:

    • Transferring or repositioning patients
    • Lifting objects from the floor
    • Reaching overhead repeatedly
    • Prolonged standing or awkward postures during procedures
    • Bed making or turning patients in bed

    Prevention of Injury:

    • Know your limits and ask for help when needed
    • Maintain physical fitness (core strength helps with lifting)
    • Be mindful of posture throughout the shift
    • Participate in training on safe patient handling

    Proper perineal care (male and female)

    General Guidelines (Applicable to Both Genders):

    • Wear clean gloves.
    • Use warm water, mild soap or perineal cleanser, and clean washcloths.
    • Provide privacy and explain the procedure to the patient.
    • Ensure proper draping to expose only the perineal area.
    • Always clean from front to back to prevent contamination.

    Perineal Care – Female:

    1. Position the patient in dorsal recumbent (lying on back with knees bent and apart) if possible.
    2. Place a waterproof pad under the hips to keep the bed dry.
    3. Expose the perineal area while maintaining privacy.
    4. Separate the labia with your non-dominant hand.
    5. With the dominant hand, clean:
      • Outer labia (majora) – wipe from front to back on one side, then use a clean wipe for the other side.
      • Inner labia (minora) – wipe each side front to back using a new part of the cloth.
      • Urethral meatus and vaginal orifice – again, front to back.
    6. Rinse the area using a clean cloth if soap was used.
    7. Pat dry gently from front to back.
    8. Apply prescribed ointments if needed.
    9. Remove gloves, perform hand hygiene, and make the patient comfortable.

    Perineal Care – Male:

    1. Position the patient in a supine position.
    2. Place a waterproof pad under the hips.
    3. Expose the perineal area while ensuring privacy.
    4. Clean the penis:
      • If uncircumcised, retract the foreskin gently.
      • Clean the tip of the penis (urethral meatus) in a circular motion, starting at the meatus and moving outward.
      • Clean the shaft of the penis from top to base.
      • Rinse and dry thoroughly.
      • Return the foreskin to its natural position to prevent paraphimosis.
      • Clean the scrotum and groin area, front to back.
    5. Clean the perineal area (between scrotum and anus), always moving from front to back.
    6. Rinse and dry thoroughly, especially in skin folds.
    7. Remove gloves, perform hand hygiene, and ensure the patient’s comfort.
      Important Reminders:
      • Document any abnormalities (redness, swelling, discharge, rashes, or foul odor).
      • Perineal care is often performed at least once daily and after voiding or defecation.
      • Use clean gloves and observe infection control at all times.

    Oral care for awake patients

    Purpose:
    • To maintain oral hygiene.
    • To prevent infection, bad breath, and dental issues.
    • To promote comfort and appetite.
    • To assess for any abnormalities (e.g., ulcers, dryness, or bleeding gums). Supplies Needed:
    • Toothbrush (soft-bristled)
    • Toothpaste
    • Cup of water
    • Emesis basin (for rinsing/spitting)
    • Towel or bib (to protect clothing)
    • Gloves (for assisted care)
    • Lip balm or moisturizer (optional)
    • Mouth mirror and tongue depressor (optional for assessment)

    Procedure for Independent Patients:

    1. Explain the procedure and offer assistance if needed.
    2. Provide all the necessary supplies on a clean surface.
    3. Ensure the patient is in a comfortable sitting or upright position.
    4. Allow the patient to:
      • Brush all tooth surfaces and tongue with gentle, circular motions.
      • Rinse mouth with water and spit into an emesis basin.
    5. Offer a towel to wipe the mouth and face.
    6. Assist or guide if the patient has difficulty with mobility or hand coordination.

    Risk assessment for fall prevention

    Fall Risk Assessment – Key Areas

    1. Patient History
      • History of previous falls (within the past 6–12 months)
      • History of dizziness, fainting, or seizures
      • Cognitive impairment (e.g., confusion, disorientation)
    2. Mobility and Gait
      • Requires assistance with walking or transferring
      • Use of assistive devices (walker, cane, wheelchair)
      • Unsteady gait or impaired balance
    3. Medications
      • Taking sedatives, antihypertensives, diuretics, or psychotropics
      • Polypharmacy (≥4 medications)
      • Recent medication changes
    4. Sensory Deficits
      • Visual or hearing impairment
      • Decreased sensation in feet or legs (neuropathy)
    5. Environmental Hazards
      • Cluttered surroundings
      • Poor lighting
      • Slippery floors or loose rugs
    6. Continence Status
      • Urinary or fecal incontinence
      • Frequent toileting needs
      • Nocturia
    7. Other Factors
      • Age ≥65 years
      • Orthostatic hypotension
      • Fatigue, weakness, or deconditioning
      • Chronic diseases (e.g., Parkinson’s, stroke, diabetes)

    Total Score Interpretation:

    • 0–24: Low risk
    • 25–44: Moderate risk
    • ≥45: High risk

    Interventions Based on Risk Level

    Low Risk:
    • Keep call bell within reach
    • Ensure proper lighting
    • Educate patient on safety
    Moderate Risk:
    • Assist with ambulation
    • Use non-skid footwear
    • Place patient near nurses’ station
    • Monitor toileting schedule
    High Risk:
  • Use bed/chair alarms
  • Maintain bed in lowest position
  • Assign frequent rounding or 1:1 sitter if needed
  • Physical therapy or mobility consult

    • Safety Measures for High-Risk Patients

    1. Environment Modification
      • Keep the bed in the lowest position with brakes locked.
      • Place frequently used items (call bell, water, tissue) within easy reach.
      • Ensure the room is clutter-free and well-lit, especially at night.
      • Use non-slip mats or flooring; remove rugs or loose wires.
    2. Fall Prevention Devices
      • Use bed and chair alarms to alert staff if the patient attempts to get up unassisted.
      • Consider side rails (only if not contraindicated) and ensure proper use.
      • Provide mobility aids (walker, cane) and ensure the patient knows how to use them.
      • Apply non-skid footwear or grip socks.
    3. Frequent Monitoring
      • Place high-risk patients close to the nurse’s station.
      • Perform hourly rounding (4Ps: Pain, Position, Potty, Possessions).
      • Use a sitter or 1:1 supervision if necessary.
    4. Medication Review
      • Collaborate with the healthcare team to review medications that increase fall or injury risk (e.g., sedatives, opioids, antihypertensives).
      • Adjust or monitor dosages as appropriate.
    5. Orientation and Communication
      • Orient patient to surroundings—show location of call bell, bathroom, and how to ask for help.
      • Place a fall risk sign outside the door or on the patient’s chart if applicable.
      • Educate family members and caregivers about the safety plan.
    6. Assist with Activities
      • Assist with toileting on a scheduled basis (prevent urgency-related falls).
      • Support with ambulation and transfers; never allow high-risk patients to walk alone.
      • Use gait belts when ambulating.
    7. Special Considerations for Cognitively Impaired Patients
      • Use visual cues (e.g., colored tape or signs).
      • Avoid physical restraints unless absolutely necessary and with MD order.
      • Encourage familiar items (photos, objects) to reduce confusion and anxiety.
    8. Documentation
      • Record all safety measures in place.
      • Monitor and document any near-miss or actual falls.
      • Report to the healthcare team and update the care plan as needed.

    Pharmacologic Pain Relief Strategies

    1. Non-Opioid Analgesics

      - Used for mild to moderate pain, often the first-line treatment.

      1. Acetaminophen (Paracetamol)
        • Mechanism: Inhibits prostaglandin synthesis in CNS.
        • Uses: Headache, muscle aches, fever.
        • Caution: Hepatotoxic in high doses.
      2. NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Diclofenac, Ketorolac)
        • Mechanism: Inhibit COX enzymes → decrease prostaglandin production.
        • Uses: Inflammatory pain (arthritis, dysmenorrhea, musculoskeletal).
        • Caution: Risk of GI ulcers, renal impairment, bleeding.
    2. Opioid Analgesics

      - Used for moderate to severe pain, acute or chronic.

      Examples:

      • Morphine, Fentanyl, Hydromorphone, Codeine, Tramadol, Oxycodone
      • Mechanism: Bind to opioid receptors in the CNS to block pain perception.
      • Uses: Postoperative pain, cancer pain, trauma.
      • Caution: Respiratory depression, sedation, constipation, risk of dependence.
    3. Adjuvant Analgesics

      - Medications not primarily for pain, but effective for specific types of pain (e.g., neuropathic, chronic pain).

      1. Antidepressants
        • Examples: Amitriptyline, Duloxetine
        • Use: Neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia
      2. Anticonvulsants
        • Examples: Gabapentin, Pregabalin
        • Use: Nerve pain (e.g., diabetic neuropathy, postherpetic neuralgia)
      3. Corticosteroids
        • Examples: Dexamethasone, Prednisone
        • Use: Inflammatory pain, cancer-related pain
        • Caution: Long-term use → risk of osteoporosis, hyperglycemia, immune suppression
      4. Muscle Relaxants
        • Examples: Baclofen, Cyclobenzaprine
        • Use: Muscle spasm-related pain
    4. Topical Analgesics

      - Used for localized pain (e.g., joints, minor injuries).

      • Examples: Lidocaine patches, Capsaicin cream, Diclofenac gel
      • Caution: Avoid contact with mucous membranes and eyes.
    5. NMDA Receptor Antagonists
      • Example: Ketamine (low-dose)
      • Use: Severe pain, opioid-tolerant patients, or as part of anesthesia

    Pain Management Considerations:

    • Assess pain regularly (intensity, location, quality, duration).
    • Use pain scales (e.g., Numeric Rating Scale, Wong-Baker Faces).
    • Start low and go slow, especially in elderly or opioid-naïve patients.
    • Consider multi-modal analgesia (combining drug classes for synergy).
    • Monitor for side effects and effectiveness.
    • Provide patient education on drug use, side effects, and safe storage.

    Non-pharmacologic pain relief (for pediatric, elderly, and pregnant women prepartum, intrapartum, postpartum)

    Non-Pharmacologic Pain Relief Strategies

    - These techniques aim to reduce pain perception, promote comfort, and enhance coping without medications. They can be used alone or alongside pharmacologic methods.

    1. Pediatric Patients (Infants to Adolescents) Infants and Toddlers:
      • Swaddling
      • Rocking or gentle movement
      • Non-nutritive sucking (pacifier)
      • Breastfeeding or sweet-tasting solutions (sucrose)
      • Skin-to-skin contact (kangaroo care)
      • Distraction with toys, mobiles, or music
      Preschoolers to Adolescents:
      • Guided imagery
      • Distraction techniques (videos, storytelling, games)
      • Deep breathing or blowing bubbles
      • Play therapy
      • Comfort positioning (parent holding during procedures)
      • Music therapy
    2. Elderly Patients
      • Thermal therapy (warm compresses for arthritis, cold packs for swelling)
      • Massage and touch therapy
      • Aromatherapy
      • Tai Chi or gentle yoga
      • Relaxation and breathing techniques
      • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)
      • Music therapy
      • Acupuncture or acupressure
      • Positioning aids for comfort in bed or chairs
      • Spiritual care or prayer (if culturally appropriate)
      3. Note: Always assess cognitive status and adapt interventions to avoid confusion or injury.
    3. Pregnant Women
      • Prepartum (During Pregnancy):
        • Prenatal yoga and stretching
        • Massage therapy
        • Warm baths or warm compresses
        • Maternity belts or support pillows
        • Breathing and relaxation exercises
        • Chiropractic care (by trained professionals)
        • Walking and light exercise
      • Intrapartum (During Labor):
        • Lamaze or patterned breathing
        • Birthing balls or peanut balls
        • Warm showers or water immersion (hydrotherapy)
        • Massage or counter-pressure (especially for back labor)
        • Visualization or meditation
        • Changing positions frequently
        • TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)
        • Doula support or partner coaching
        • Music or aromatherapy
      • Postpartum:
        • Perineal cold packs for vaginal delivery discomfort
        • Warm compresses for uterine cramps
        • Massage or gentle movement
        • Breastfeeding support and positioning (to ease nipple pain)
        • Guided relaxation to manage stress and fatigue
        • Supportive positioning during infant care
        • Spiritual or emotional support

    General Benefits of Non-Pharmacologic Pain Relief:

    • No side effects or drug interactions.
    • Empowers the patient and supports holistic care.
    • Promotes comfort, trust, and a sense of control.
